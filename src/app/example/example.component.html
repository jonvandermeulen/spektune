This is a frozen moment in time - a transect across a beautiful swirling eddy off the coast of the Kamchatka Peninsula in the Western Bering Sea. Collected by the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) onboard the Aqua satellite.

  • rrs-443 - Double Bass - A-minor pentatonic (relative minor of C-maj), 02-04 octave
  • rrs-547 - Harp - C-major pentatonic, 03-05 octave
  • rrs-667 - Flute - C-major pentatonic, 04-06 octave
From Ryan Vandermeulen:
Something kind of fun about the eddy: you can see that it is rotating clockwise in the image (thus it is an anticyclonic, or "warm core" eddy). Due to the effect of the Coriolis Force (i.e. the physical force imparted on the water by a spinning Earth), water sort of piles up in the middle when they turn clockwise, and the opposite happens when they spin counter clockwise (i.e. there is a shallow depression in sea surface height, by 10 cm or so).

Analogous to weather patterns, water moves from areas of high pressure (or sea surface height) to areas of low pressure (sea surface height). If you imagine that you could roll a golf ball starting from the center of the eddy, it would "roll downhill", or towards the outer edge of the eddy, but because the Earth is spinning, the ball would move a little bit to the right (in the direction of rotation). All the particles of water are being pushed slightly to the right as the pressure gradient force (movement from high to low height) and the Coriolis force (spin of Earth) are balanced (this is called Geostrophic Balance).

As I mentioned, it is spinning, and all the water is being pushed to the right, and those particles of water that were pushed right, they push the water beneath them a little to the right as well, and so forth, and thus, water continues to "pile up" in the middle, sustaining the forces of rotation. In addition, the pile up of surface water means that below the surface of the ocean, your access to deep sea nutrients is also being pushed down farther.

Notice that the middle of the eddy is blue = less nutrients = less phytoplankton. The opposite happens when the rotation is counter-clockwise ("cold-core" eddy). In that case, as water is pushed to the right, it's not piling up, but moving outward, and since that water void needs to be replaced from somewhere, and it is essentially "pumped up" from the deeper, colder ocean, which is full of rich nutrients that facilitate phytoplankton growth

This spectral reflectance data are extracted from a rolling 32-day average global image from MODIS-Aqua, and the averages are computed every 8-days, making for 48 images per year.

The actual data that are extracted are actually the mean of a 10x10 box of 9 km resolution data (which equals a box of 8, 100 km2), in order to help fill in gaps that may arise from cloud cover throughout the year. The center location of that 10x10 box is -16.625, 153.2917

With so few data points used to express an entire year, we slowed this one down to around 20 BPM and used synth instruments with long, sweeping depth. You can really hear the how the seasons change (eg: listen to 0:45 - 1:30).

A series of daily images captured by the Visual Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite (VIIRS), from August 1-31, 2016.

Each 'beat' is a dotted-quarter note. Each track is offset from the next one by a 16th note, leaving an 8th note 'rest' in between each row of data. The result is a kind of somber waltz.

  • rrs410 and rrs671 are Acoustic Guitar. They bookend each phrase.
  • rrs443 is a Marimba, and rrs551 is a Vibraphone. They are similar but distinct instruments, and they form the interior of each phrase.
  • rrs486 is a thumping standup bass at the center of each phrase.

The song is the same data set repeated four times. Each repetition, a different color takes center stage and performs a solo of sorts while the other colors provide background.

In the first part, blue light (Rrs-443 and Rrs-469) rapidly picks an acoustic guitar, while green light (harp) and red light (bass) provide a rhythmic backing.

In the second part, blue takes a back seat and red (Rrs-667) takes its turn thumping out a bass solo.

In the third part, (Rrs-547 and Rrs-555) green shines.

The data set was rather large - over 200 data points for each measured wavelength of light (there are 10 - 4 blue, 3 green, 3 red). We chose 2 blue, 2 green and 1 red - so about a thousand data points - it's too much to play all at once. When the instruments are in "background" mode, their data is averaged over 4 points. So in each repetition, they are playing the same part, but just with a much simpler data set spread over a narrower set of notes. When in "solo" mode, every data point is played and we open up the range of notes it can play.

The result is a subjective interpretation of objective data. We did not manipulate the data in any way that is not supported by science - Just like NASA uses data coming in from it's satellites to produce false-color images of the earth and space, to show us something our puny human eyeballs could never see.

If the song sounds like it ends arbitrarily - like in the middle of something much bigger - that's because it is. It's based entirely off an arbitrary line drawn on a map... and the map goes on forever.

This song is built from data of the price of Bitcoin from May 17, 2021 - May 17, 2022.

  • Plucky ethereal synth is the price of BTC at close.
  • Bassline is the absolute value of change - a kind of basic measure of volatility. If the price goes up or down significantly, the pitch of the bass rises.
  • The babbling noisy thing in the background is volume of trade.
  • The drum track is nothing but a preset - no relation to the data. (still working on finding a good way to do drum rhythms with data)
