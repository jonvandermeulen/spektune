About Spektune

An application for translating data into MIDI files (and ultimately, into music). The concept was inspired by and developed in collaboration with Ryan Vandermeulen, Satellite Oceanographer at NASA Goddard's Ocean Ecology Laboratory.

Simply paste a list of numbers into the system, and listen to the results. Change the key, the scales, the timing, the instruments... See where it takes you! Spektune Composer has a built-in player based on ToneJS and Benjamin Gleitzman's pre-rendered soudnfonts.

For best results, use Spektune to generate a MIDI file and import it into any Digial Audio Workstation. There are some really great free options available. All of our sample songs were produced in GarageBand.

How it started

Our original plan was to turn reflectance data gathered by NASA satellites into music, in order to highlight the natural harmonies of blue/green/red light being reflected (or absorbed) by our ocean the microscopic life in it.

Initial attempts proved interesting, but suffered from poor-quality instruments and limited expression options.

How it's going

Spektune Composer was developed to help us experiment and blend musical theory with raw numbers - in a way that turns objective science into subjective beauty.

Anyone can use this tool. We would love to hear what you come up with! Share it with us @Spektune on Twitter and Instagram.

How it works

Generate a MIDI track by supplying data and selecting configuration options for the musical interpreter.

Select a key/scale that you want the data to conform to, along with an octave range. Provide a list of line-break-delimited numbers. Spektune will normalize the data and map each data point to a note on the selected key/scale/octaves. Options can be specified to govern the performance of the data. For instance, tie repeated notes together to form one longer note.

Once the data is processed by this tool, you can import the resulting MIDI file into any Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) software, where you can further develop the song.

About the Authors

Ryan Vandermeulen is a Satellite Oceanographer at NASA Goddard's Ocean Ecology Laboratory.

"The ocean is blue... sort of," Ryan explains, "but the color palette of the ocean is much more than just blue." NASA satellites measure light reflected from the ocean's surface, using instruments that detect specific wavelengths of light - like red, green, and blue (albeit in a much more complicated science-y way). The intensity of these colors, and their relative values to each other tell us about the balance of microscopic life in the ocean - phytoplankton that are responsible for generating much of the oxygen that we breathe.

The data can look chaotic.

"Nothing is linear in nature, and nothing is predictable beyond first order tendencies. But within the chaos is an overtly sophisticated order that we owe our lives to."

Ryan created a prototype song developed from a transect (sequential data that represents a line drawn across the map, as it were) of the Gulf of Mexico.

He worked with his brother, Jon, to turn the data into music. See the results here.

"These melodies are simply a manifestation of natural patterns created by microscopic life in the ocean."

Ryan lives near Washington, D.C.. When he is not doing science in the lab, he is doing science in the kitchen and makes keen observations of the natural world with his young daughter.

Jon Vandermeulen is a veteran software engineer, with over 25 years experience developing web applications.

"When Ryan emailed me his idea and a (really, quite terrible) audio rendering, I told him that I was going to help him and he didn't have a choice about that," says Jon.

"Our first attempt used a browser-based audio engine called ToneJS - which is awesome for what it is (a simple-ish way to play instruments in a web browser), but not awesome for what it isn't (a studio-quality Digital Audio Workstation)."

Over the course of 18 months, the project evolved into a system for generating note data that could be imported into any DAW via Musical Instrument Digital Interface (MIDI) files.

"Music has always been a part of our lives," Jon says. "Ryan and I both spent some time nursing delusions of Rock-Stardom, but we never seriously collaborated on anything."

Jon lives in Houston, TX. When he is not engineering software, he is being raised by his wife and three teen children.
